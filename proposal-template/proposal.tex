\section{Application domain and motivation}
% ============================================================

The project is a \textbf{Chess Club Tournament \& Rating Manager}: a small,
self-hosted system that lets a chess club run Swiss-system tournaments, record
game results, maintain Elo ratings over time and answer performance questions
across a player's whole game history. Small clubs today typically juggle a
spreadsheet for the member list, a second spreadsheet per tournament for the
pairings and results, and no persistent record of ratings or openings played --
which makes any resembling question (``how does player X score with Black
in the Sicilian?'') practically unanswerable. Consolidating this into a single
relational store solves that.

Two concrete usage scenarios illustrate the intended use:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Tournament director, Friday evening.} The director opens a new
        four-round Swiss tournament, registers twelve players from the existing
        member list, records the results of round~1 through the frontend and
        prints the standings after each round. Elo ratings update automatically
        when the tournament is closed.
  \item \textbf{Club member, at home.} A player opens the frontend, points it
        at the club server and looks up their personal statistics: overall
        win/draw/loss counts, score by colour, score per opening and Elo
        progression over the last twelve months.
\end{itemize}

\section{Data model -- ER sketch}
% ============================================================

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{assets/figure_1.png}
  \caption{ER sketch of the chess-club data model: entity types, key attributes,
  relationships and cardinalities. The central N:M relationship is
  \emph{player~$\leftrightarrow$~tournament}, realised through the associative
  entity \texttt{registration}.}
  \label{fig:er}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:er} shows the data model. The central entity types are
\texttt{player} (name, birth year, current Elo), \texttt{tournament} (name,
start date, number of rounds, status), \texttt{round} (round number within a
tournament), \texttt{game} (the associative entity linking two players, one
round and one opening, plus the result), \texttt{opening} (ECO code, name) and
\texttt{rating\_history} (Elo value of a player at a point in time). The
mandatory \textbf{N:M relationship} is
\emph{player~$\leftrightarrow$~tournament}: a player takes part in many
tournaments over time, and a tournament has many players. It is resolved by the
associative entity \texttt{registration}, which carries the seed and the final
rank. The \texttt{game} entity itself is a second, richer N:M associative
entity, linking two players (white and black) with an opening within a round.
The relationship
\emph{tournament~$\rightarrow$~round~$\rightarrow$~game} is a strict 1:N chain,
and \emph{player~$\rightarrow$~rating\_history} is 1:N as well.

% ============================================================
\section{From model to schema}
% ============================================================

Applying the ER-to-relational mapping rules to Figure~\ref{fig:er} 
yields a schema of seven tables:
each strong entity becomes a table, the N:M relationship
\texttt{plays\_in} becomes its own table \texttt{registration}
carrying the relationship attributes \texttt{seed} and
\texttt{final\_rank}, and the remaining 1:N relationships
become foreign keys on the N-side.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{player}(\underline{id}, name, birth\_year, current\_elo)
  \item \texttt{opening}(\underline{eco\_code}, name)
  \item \texttt{tournament}(\underline{id}, name, start\_date, num\_rounds, status)
  \item \texttt{registration}(\underline{\emph{player\_id}, \emph{tournament\_id}}, seed, final\_rank)
  \item \texttt{round}(\underline{id}, \emph{tournament\_id}, round\_no),
        \textsc{unique}(tournament\_id, round\_no)
  \item \texttt{game}(\underline{id}, \emph{round\_id}, \emph{white\_id}, \emph{black\_id},
        \emph{eco\_code}, result), \\
        \hspace*{1em}\textsc{check}(result IN (\texttt{'1-0'}, \texttt{'0-1'}, \texttt{'1/2-1/2'})), \\
        \hspace*{1em}\textsc{check}(white\_id $\neq$ black\_id)
  \item \texttt{rating\_history}(\underline{id}, \emph{player\_id}, valid\_from, elo)
\end{itemize}

Foreign keys: \texttt{registration.player\_id}~$\to$~\texttt{player},
\texttt{registration.tournament\_id}~$\to$~\texttt{tournament},
\texttt{round.tournament\_id}~$\to$~\texttt{tournament},
\texttt{game.round\_id}~$\to$~\texttt{round},
\texttt{game.white\_id}, \texttt{game.black\_id}~$\to$~\texttt{player},
\texttt{game.eco\_code}~$\to$~\texttt{opening},
\texttt{rating\_history.player\_id}~$\to$~\texttt{player}.

\textbf{Normalisation.} Every non-key attribute depends on the whole primary key
and only on it: opening names live in \texttt{opening}, not repeated in
\texttt{game}; a player's current Elo is stored once in \texttt{player} and its
history is factored into \texttt{rating\_history}. There are no transitive
dependencies, so the schema is in \textbf{3NF}.

\section{API design}
% ============================================================
The FastAPI layer exposes read endpoints for public statistics and standings and
write endpoints (protected by \texttt{X-API-Key}) for anything that mutates
club data.

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}p{1.6cm}p{4.6cm}X@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{Path} & \textbf{Purpose} \\
\midrule
\texttt{GET}  & \texttt{/players}                              & list of players with total games played, computed as \texttt{COUNT(*)} over \texttt{game} where the player appears as \texttt{white\_id} or \texttt{black\_id} (\emph{JOIN + aggregation}) \\
\texttt{GET}  & \texttt{/players/\{id\}/stats}                 & win/draw/loss and average Elo delta grouped by \texttt{opening.name} (\emph{JOIN game~$\bowtie$~opening + aggregation}) \\
\texttt{GET}  & \texttt{/tournaments}                          & list of all tournaments \\
\texttt{GET}  & \texttt{/tournaments/\{id\}/standings}         & points per player + Buchholz tiebreak (\emph{JOIN across 4 tables + aggregation}) \\
\texttt{POST} & \texttt{/players}                              & create a new player (\emph{X-API-Key}) \\
\texttt{POST} & \texttt{/tournaments}                          & create a new tournament (\emph{X-API-Key}) \\
\texttt{POST} & \texttt{/tournaments/\{id\}/registrations}     & register a player (\emph{X-API-Key}) \\
\texttt{POST} & \texttt{/games}                                & record a game result, triggers Elo update (\emph{X-API-Key}) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}

The signature endpoint is \texttt{GET /tournaments/\{id\}/standings}: it JOINs
\texttt{registration} to \texttt{player} on \texttt{player\_id}, and
\texttt{game} to \texttt{round} on \texttt{round\_id} (filtered by
\texttt{round.tournament\_id}), then groups by player to compute
\texttt{SUM(points)} plus the Buchholz sum of the opponents' scores. All four
\texttt{POST} endpoints require the \texttt{X-API-Key} header.

\section{Operation with Docker Compose}
% ============================================================

The \texttt{docker-compose.yml} defines two services: \texttt{postgres}
(official \texttt{postgres:16} image, with the schema and seed data mounted as
an init script under \texttt{/docker-entrypoint-initdb.d}) and \texttt{api}
(the FastAPI application, built from a local \texttt{Dockerfile}). Credentials
-- \texttt{POSTGRES\_USER}, \texttt{POSTGRES\_PASSWORD}, \texttt{POSTGRES\_DB}
and \texttt{API\_KEY} -- live in a \texttt{.env} file that is
\texttt{.gitignore}-d. Deployment on the lecture server is one
step: clone the repository, place \texttt{.env} beside the compose file and run
\texttt{docker compose up -d}. The frontend \texttt{.deb} is installed
separately on the same server via \texttt{sudo dpkg -i chess-club-fe.deb} and
connects to the API over HTTP.

\section{Scope, milestones and risks}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Scope.} 7 tables, 8 endpoints (4 read, 4 write), 4 tkinter screens plus
the connection dialog.

\textbf{Milestones and schedule.}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}p{1.4cm}Xp{1.8cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Week} & \textbf{Milestone} & \textbf{Est. hours} \\
\midrule
1     & Schema, DDL, seed data (players + one closed tournament) ready in PostgreSQL & 8 \\
2--3  & FastAPI: read + write endpoints, \texttt{X-API-Key} dependency, standings query with JOIN + aggregation & 12 \\
4--5  & tkinter frontend (4 screens + connection dialog); \texttt{.deb} builds and installs on the lecture server & 10 \\
6     & \texttt{pytest} test suite, documentation (LaTeX repo with CI), 8--10 min video & 10 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}

\textbf{Risks.} (1)~The Elo update on \texttt{POST /games} is a small business
rule with real edge cases (both players' ratings must move symmetrically, and
recording a game against an unregistered player must be refused). Getting this
subtly wrong is the most likely way to fail correctness tests.
(2)~Packaging the tkinter frontend as a working \texttt{.deb} on the lecture
server is a build-system risk unrelated to the database itself.

\section{Acceptance criteria and testing}
% ============================================================

The system counts as done and correct when the criteria below hold on the
lecture server, verified by an automated test suite.

\textbf{Acceptance criteria.}
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt]
  \item A \texttt{POST /games} with \texttt{white\_id = black\_id} is rejected
        with HTTP~400 (\textsc{check} constraint).
  \item A \texttt{POST /games} whose \texttt{result} is not in
        \texttt{\{'1-0','0-1','1/2-1/2'\}} is rejected with HTTP~400.
  \item Registering the same player twice for the same tournament is rejected
        with HTTP~409 (primary key on \texttt{registration}).
  \item Recording a game for a player who is not registered in that
        tournament is rejected with HTTP~409.
  \item \texttt{GET /tournaments/\{id\}/standings} returns the correct
        \texttt{SUM(points)} per player and Buchholz tiebreak (JOIN +
        aggregation).
  \item Every \texttt{POST} endpoint without a valid \texttt{X-API-Key}
        returns HTTP~401.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Test plan.}
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Database:} PK on all tables, composite PK on
        \texttt{registration}, FKs on \texttt{round}, \texttt{game},
        \texttt{registration}, \texttt{rating\_history}, \textsc{unique} on
        \texttt{(tournament\_id, round\_no)}, \textsc{check} on
        \texttt{game.result} and on \texttt{white\_id $\neq$ black\_id},
        \textsc{not null} on all foreign keys.
  \item \textbf{API:} \texttt{pytest} with FastAPI's \texttt{TestClient}
        against a throw-away PostgreSQL container. Happy paths
        for all four \texttt{GET} endpoints and all four \texttt{POST}
        endpoints, plus 4xx error cases for each acceptance criterion above.
  \item \textbf{Business rule (risk 1).} Dedicated test that records a game
        for a player \emph{not} registered in the tournament and asserts
        HTTP~409; a second test records a legal game and asserts that both
        players' Elo values change symmetrically and that a
        \texttt{rating\_history} row is written for each.
\end{itemize}
